Types of Agricultural Jobs

Laborers: Laborers perform physical work in agriculturalfields and farms, such as lumbering, preparing soil, planting seeds, spreadingfertilizer and harvesting crops. On the other hand, those who work in fisheriesand ranches feed and take care of fish or animals and may also milk cows,gather eggs or shear sheep.
Agricultural Salesperson: This professional sells farm machinery, equipment and other farm supplies to farmers. The major components of their job include visiting various farms and marketing products at agricultural shows.
Foresters: Foresters manage the national forests. These professionals oversee natural resources of forests, such as trees, minerals, water and wildlife. They must take care of the economic, environmental, recreational and conservational well being of range lands and forests. Some of the common job titles include arborist, naturalist,forester, forest nursery manager or forestry district ranger.
Agricultural Economist: This professional advises farm owners on efficient ways to make their agricultural enterprise more profitable. They study all aspects of livestock and food production as well as the production process.
Agronomist: The agronomists deal with the science of cultivating crops by investigating various aspects such as fertilization, irrigation, crop management and harvesting. Educational establishments and manufacturers of agriculture products often use this service.
Agricultural Inspector: Agricultural inspectors work for the government to enforce agricultural laws and regulations. Their work includes collecting samples from animals, plants and agricultural products; interpreting regulations to agricultural workers and farmers; ensuring good hygienic conditions at meat processing plants and slaughterhouses; submitting written findings; and closing facilities that do not comply with the set regulations.
Agricultural Manager: These agricultural jobs involve coordinating the operation of agricultural establishments, such as farms,ranches, nurseries and timber tracts. They watch the market price of various agricultural products and use that information to determine the crops to grow and the animals to raise. Moreover, they hire, train, and supervise the laborers who perform the daily physical tasks in the farm, such as planting, pruning, watering and harvesting plants as well as feeding and taking care of animals.
Agricultural Engineer: These experts are responsible for designing various equipment and tools that make the agricultural world more efficient.The work of agricultural engineers can involve the development and renovation of anything from tractors to grain storage facilities.
Agricultural Scientists: Agricultural scientists come up with ideas on how to improve agricultural methods by researching on farm crops and animals. They can conduct basic studies on the chemical and biological processes of crop livestock and crop growth or applied studies in which they monitor the safety of food products. Food scientists use their knowledge in sciences, such chemistry and biotechnology, to work with food industries and the government on how to improve food production. Common job titles under this category include soil scientists, plant scientists or animal scientists.
Extension Specialist: This career usually requires at least a masterâ€™s degree in agriculture. Extension specialists work with extension agents to conduct training programs on agricultural matters. These professionals update local extension agents on recent news pertaining to their respective agricultural region. The specialists can also develop materials for extension agents to circulate so as to inform the public about new developments and how to implement them.
Agricultural journalist: Farmers need to be updated on the latest news and developments in agricultural field. Advances in research, commodity price fluctuations and changes in government regulations are very important to growers.Agricultural journalists write or broadcast on issues related to food production. Such professionals can work at print publications, radio and television stations, commodity organizations and farm machinery industries.Similarly, agricultural photographers shoot photos of crops, livestock and farms and attach them to magazines and newspapers, or post them online.
